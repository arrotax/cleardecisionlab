Before you do anything else

Know which category your symptoms belong to.

Not all cognitive changes in your 50s are the same. The category determines the first action. Most women have never been told the difference.

✓

Normal aging

Slower word retrieval, occasional tip-of-the-tongue, mildly slower processing. Frustrating. Not dangerous. Not a signal to act on urgently.

!

Reversible contributors — the most important category

Sleep apnea, hypothyroidism, depression, certain medications. Produce symptoms identical to cognitive decline. Frequently missed. Entirely treatable. Identifying these is the highest-priority first action for most women.

!

Vascular cognitive impairment

Driven by chronic hypertension, small vessel disease, blood sugar dysregulation. Accumulates silently for years. Directly preventable through specific, measurable actions.

↓

Alzheimer's continuum

From preclinical accumulation to mild impairment to dementia. The one most women fear. Also the one with the longest preventable runway — and the one where the actions in this guide have the most impact, taken now.

The Nun Study — 678 Catholic sisters, tracked for decades
"Some sisters had brains riddled with Alzheimer's pathology on autopsy — and zero cognitive symptoms during life. They had built enough cognitive reserve that the disease could not express itself."
Snowdon et al., JAMA, 1996 — the most cited evidence for cognitive reserve as a buildable, protective system

What the research actually says

Four things most women believe — and what the evidence shows instead.

❌ Myth
"Brain games and crosswords protect against dementia."
✓ Evidence
Crosswords make you better at crosswords. The ACTIVE trial found speed-of-processing training — not puzzle-solving — associated with lower dementia incidence at 20-year follow-up. One specific tool. Not most apps.
❌ Myth
"Omega-3 supplements are the best brain protection strategy."
✓ Evidence
The WHO explicitly recommends against supplementation as a dementia prevention strategy. Dietary patterns have stronger evidence. Treating your blood pressure has stronger evidence.
❌ Myth
"Cognitive decline after 50 is mostly genetics — there is not much you can do."
✓ Evidence
Pure familial Alzheimer's is a small fraction of cases. Up to 45% of dementia cases are estimated to be preventable through modifiable factors. Genetics is not the dominant variable for most women.
❌ Myth
"Brain fog in perimenopause is just hormones — wait it out."
✓ Evidence
Hypothyroidism, sleep apnea, B12 deficiency, and medication effects produce identical symptoms — and are frequently missed. The first priority is ruling out treatable contributors, not waiting for hormone levels to stabilize.
