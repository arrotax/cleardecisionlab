Why generic advice doesn't work

Imagine going to a doctor with chest pain. And they say: "Everyone benefits from aspirin."

The problem is that chest pain can be a symptom of angina, intercostal neuralgia, or heartburn. And the treatment in each case is different.

Burnout is the same.

One person is physically depleted: their nervous system is stuck in survival mode, they sleep 7 hours but wake up exhausted.

Another is cognitively overloaded: their brain switches between tasks 47 times a day, and they have no processing power left for decision-making.

A third is emotionally exhausted: their motivation system has shut down because they're doing work that conflicts with their values.

All three are "burned out." But if you give the first one meditation, the second one better sleep, and the third one time management, nothing will change.

Because the treatment doesn't match the diagnosis.

What's actually "leaking" in your system

When you feel burnout, one of three systems is leaking:

Physical. Your body is stuck in constant mobilization mode. You're not recovering, even when you rest. Sleep doesn't refresh you. Energy doesn't return.
Cognitive. Your brain is fragmented. You switch between tasks, check your phone, respond to messages—and you have no resources left for deep work and strategic decisions.
Emotional. Your motivation system has shut down. You're doing what you're supposed to do, but it doesn't align with what matters to you. And every day of this misalignment drains you further.

These aren't metaphors. These are measurable systems with specific restoration protocols backed by 50 years of organizational psychology, neuroscience, and clinical research.

But you can't apply the right protocol until you know which system is bleeding the most.
