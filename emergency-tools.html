3 Emergency Protocols When You're Stuck

Quick interventions for when you're frozen, spiraling, or paralyzed by overthinking

Important: These protocols will help you today. They will not change your baseline.

They're designed to interrupt the pattern in the moment—not prevent it from happening again tomorrow.

Think of them as circuit breakers. They give you traction when you have none. But they don't fix the wiring.

When to Use These

You're at your desk. The task is clear. But something stops you from starting.

Or: your mind is racing through worst-case scenarios and you can't access rational thought.

Or: you've been "deciding" on something for days and you're no closer to an answer.

These protocols are for those exact moments.

They don't solve the underlying problem. They give you enough space to move.

PROTOCOL 1

When Anxiety Spirals: The Present-Tense Reset

Anxiety feeds on predictions. Your brain treats "what if I fail" the same as "I am failing." This forces your nervous system back to what's actually happening right now.

  1. Stop what you're doing
  2. Ask yourself out loud: "What do I know for certain right now?"
  3. Answer only with present-tense facts: "I'm sitting here. I'm breathing. Nothing bad is happening right now."
  4. Repeat until the chest tightness loosens (60-90 seconds)

Your brain can't argue with present reality. Anxiety loses fuel when trapped in facts.

What this doesn't do:

It doesn't prevent the next spiral. It doesn't address why your baseline anxiety is high. It gives you 10-15 minutes of clarity—enough to act, not enough to rebuild the pattern.

PROTOCOL 2

When You Can't Start: The 2-Minute Permission

Your brain overestimates the pain of starting. The gap between "knowing what to do" and "doing it" creates resistance. This protocol removes the commitment.

  1. Set a timer for exactly 2 minutes
  2. Start the task—any entry point
  3. When the timer goes off, you have permission to stop
  4. (Most people continue. Momentum takes over.)

You're not committing to finishing. You're committing to 2 minutes. That removes most of the friction.

What this doesn't do:

It doesn't fix why starting feels so heavy in the first place. It doesn't address the friction itself—just bypasses it temporarily. Tomorrow, you'll need the same trick again.

PROTOCOL 3

When You Can't Decide: The 70% Rule

Most decisions are reversible. But your system treats them as permanent. This creates paralysis. The 70% rule breaks that pattern.

  1. Ask: "Is this decision reversible?"
  2. Ask: "Do I have 70% of the information I need?"
  3. If yes to both → decide in the next 60 seconds
  4. If no → you need more data, not more time thinking

Waiting for 100% certainty guarantees you'll miss the window. Most decisions don't need perfection—they need movement.

What this doesn't do:

It doesn't reduce your need for certainty. It doesn't address why your brain inflates the stakes of every choice. It works once, maybe twice—but without changing the underlying decision-making system, you'll overthink the next one just as much.

Why These Protocols Aren't Enough

If you're using these daily—if you need emergency resets constantly—that's the signal.

You don't have an execution problem. You have a systems problem.

Emergency protocols treat symptoms. They stop the fire. They don't prevent the next one.

And if your baseline is: high anxiety, chronic avoidance, decision paralysis—emergency tools become a dependency, not a solution.

The Real Question

Do you want tools you have to remember to use when things go wrong?

Or do you want a system that reduces how often things go wrong in the first place?

Emergency protocols are reactive. They help when you're already stuck.

Prevention systems are proactive. They lower your baseline anxiety, reduce friction automatically, and streamline decisions before they pile up.

The difference:

Emergency = putting out fires.
Prevention = fireproofing the building.

These three protocols work. But if you're using the same tools every day, you don't need better tools—you need different infrastructure.

What Prevention Looks Like

A prevention system isn't more techniques. It's a different approach:

Instead of resetting anxiety when it spikes → you stabilize your baseline so it spikes less often.

Instead of forcing yourself to start → you reduce the friction that makes starting feel heavy.

Instead of making faster decisions under pressure → you build frameworks that remove the pressure entirely.

That's the difference between emergency tools and an operating system.

One keeps you functional when things break. The other prevents most of the breaks.
